If we learn more information about an event, how should we update the probability of that event?

This idea can be represented with **conditional probability**, which is the probability of an event $A$ happening *given that* an event $B$ happened. We write this as $P(A | B)$.

## Definition

**Conditional probability** the ratio of the probability of both $A$ and $B$ occurring simultaneously and the probability of $B$:

$$
P(A | B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}
$$

<Image src="/content/courses/probability/assets/conditional.png" invert />

## Independence

To know that conditional probability would be useful in a situation, we must know if the events are *dependent*. So, the events $A$ and $B$ are **independent** if and only if

$$
P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)
$$

If $A$ and $B$ are independent, then $P(A | B) = P(A)$ would also be true.

## Bayes' theorem

Since $P(A \cap B) = P(A | B)P(B) = P(B | A)P(A)$ by the product rule, we can also write that

$$
P(A | B) = \frac{P(B | A)P(A)}{P(B)}
$$

to arrive at Bayes' theorem.

Bayes' theorem can do many things that the definition of conditional probability by itself cannot. There's even an entire field of statistics about it (Bayesian statistics)!

A simple use case is reversing a conditional probability. For example, if you know $P(\text{positive test} | \text{disease})$ and want $P(\text{disease} | \text{positive test})$.

This theorem has important applications in machine learning too. In <Link href="/glossary#supervised-learning">supervised learning</Link>, it's used in the naive Bayes algorithm for classification.
