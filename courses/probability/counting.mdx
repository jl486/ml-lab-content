## Basic counting principles

### multiplicative principle

The multiplicative princple states that if a process has $k$ stages with $n_1, n_2, \dots, n_k$ possible outcomes for each stage respectively, then the process can occur in $n_1 n_2 \cdots n_k$ ways.

For example, how do we find how many different 6-digit passwords are possible? Each digit is from 0-9. Because there are 6 times we select a digit, and that digit has 10 different possibilities, the total number of possible passwords is $10^6$.

### Factorial

The factorial of $n$ is the product of all positive integers less than or equal to $n$:

$$
n! = n \cdot (n - 1) \cdot (n - 2) \cdot \cdots \cdot 2 \cdot 1
$$

This is useful because it gives us the number of permutations (number of ways you can arrange) of all $n$ items.

For example, suppose you have 4 books and want to arrange all of them in a shelf. You have 4 choices for the book in the first position. After choosing a book already, you now have 3 choices for the second position. Then you have 2 choices, then 1. Therefore, there are $4! = 4 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1$ ways to arrange the 4 books.

## Permutations

If you have $n$ distinct elements, and you want to choose and arrange $k$ of them, where different orders count as different outcomes, then the number of permutations

$$
P(n, k) = \frac{n!}{(n - k)!}
$$

### Derivation

Imagine you have $k$ blank slots that you want to fill with objects from a collection of $n$ distinct objects. You have $n$ choices for the first slot. Since $n - 1$ now remain, you have $n - 1$ choices for the second slot. For the $k$-th object, you'll have $n - k + 1$ choices. By the multiplicative principle, the number of ways we can choose objects is

$$
P(n, k) = n(n - 1)(n - 2)  \cdots (n - k + 1)
$$

Multiply the numerator and denominator by $(n - k)!$ to get

$$
P(n, k) = \frac{n(n - 1)(n - 2)  \cdots (n - k + 1)(n - k)!}{(n - k!)}
$$

The numerator is the definition of $n!$, so we finally get

$$
P(n, k) = \frac{n!}{(n - k)!}
$$

## Combinations

The number of combinations is how many ways you can choose $k$ items from a collection of $n$ items.

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 
n \\
k
\end{pmatrix} = \frac{n!}{k!(n - k)!}
$$

### Derivation

We can derive this by expressing the number of $k$-permutations of $n$ items in terms of combinations.

First, we select $k$ items for the permutation. There's
$\big(\begin{smallmatrix} 
n \\
k
\end{smallmatrix}\big)$
ways to do this. After that, we know there are $k!$ ways to permute each choice of those $k$ items. Then, we have another permutation formula:

$$
P(n, k) =
\begin{pmatrix} 
n \\
k
\end{pmatrix}
\cdot k!
$$

Dividing both sides by $k!$ will then get us the combination formula.

## Partitions

Suppose you have 120 distinctly-labeled apples and are distributing them evenly into 10 boxes. In how many ways can you do this?

Since each box gets the same number of apples, each box will have $\frac{120}{10} = 12$ apples. We also know that there are $120!$ ways to arrange all the apples. Because we don't care about the order of the apples within each box, we must also divide by $12!$ for each box. The number of ways is then:

$$
\frac{120!}{(12!)^{10}}
$$

### General case

The number of ways we can partition $n$ distinct items into $k$ groups of sizes $n_1, n_2, \dots, n_k$ is

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 
n \\
n_1, n_2, \dots, n_k
\end{pmatrix} =
\frac{n!}{n_1!n_2! \cdots n_k!}
$$

This is also called the *multinomial coefficient*.

## Discrete uniform distributions

When every outcome of event $A$ is equally likely, the situation is called a *discrete uniform distribution*. In this case,

$$
P(E) = \frac{\text{number of outcomes in event}~A}{\text{number of outcomes in sample space}~\Omega}
$$

This means that counting alone is enough to calculate probabilities.
