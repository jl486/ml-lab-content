It would be useful if we were able to refer to an outcome of an experiment. For example, we may want to mathematically represent a problem like the sum of two die rolls. To do this, we could let $X_1$ and $X_2$ be the outcomes of the dice ($1$, $2$, $3$, $4$, $5$, and $6$). The expression 

$$
X_1 + X_2
$$

would represent the sum of the two die rolls. Here, $X_1$ and $X_2$ are *random variables*. A **random variable**, put simply, is a function whose output value is an outcome of a particular experiment.

## Types

Random variables can be either *discrete* or *continuous*.

* **Discrete random variables** have values that are countable or countably infinite. For example, The outcome of a die roll is a discrete random variable.
* **Continuous random variables** can take on any value in a continuous range, such as height or duration.

## Distribution functions

A **distribution function** describes how the probabilities are spread out over the possible values of a random variable.

### Discrete case

For a discrete random variable $X$, its distribution is given by the **probability mass function**, which takes in a value $x$ and tells you the probability of the random variable $X$ being equal to that value:

$$
p_X(x) = P(X = x)
$$

A PMF must satisfy these conditions:

* non-negativity: $p_X(x) \gt 0$ for all $x \in \Omega$
* total probability: $\sum_{x \in \Omega} p_X(x) = 1$

What if we wanted to find the probability of outcomes in an interval or below a certain threshold? We can find these probabilities with the **cumulative distribution function**, which adds up the probabilities of all possible values of $X$ that is at or below a specific threshold:

$$
F_X(x) = P(X \leq x) = \sum_{x_i \leq x} P(X = x_i)
$$

#### Example

Consider a fair six-sided die. Let $X$ be the outcome of the die roll. The sample space will then be $\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$. Since $P(X = x)$ is $\frac{1}{6}$ for all $x \in \Omega$, the PMF for $X$ is

$$
p_X(x) = \frac{1}{6}
$$

and the CDF of $X$ is

$$
F_X(x) = \sum_{x_i \leq x} \frac{1}{6}
$$

### Continuous case

The ideas related to discrete random variables are pretty similar to its continuous counterpart. Except, continuous random variables don't have a PMF because the probability that the variable equals a single value is zero.

Instead of a PMF, continuous random variables have a **probability density function**. This function describes how the probability is spread out rather than at singular values. Let's define its PDF $f_X(x)$ as the probability at a *very tiny* interval around $x$ divided by the length of the interval (to get its height):

$$
f_X(x) = \lim_{\delta \to 0} \frac{P(x \leq X \leq x + \delta)}{\delta}
$$

Since the CDF $F_X(x) = P(X \leq x)$, $P(x \leq X \leq X + \delta) = F(x + \delta) - F(x)$, and we can perform a substitution and see that:

$$
f_X(x) = \lim_{\delta \to 0} \frac{F(x + \delta) - F(x)}{\delta} = \frac{dF_X(x)}{dx}
$$

given that $F_X(x)$ is differentiable at $x$. Thus, the PDF is the derivative of the CDF.

The PDF must also satisfy non-negativity and the conditions for total probability:

* $f_X(x) \geq 0$
* $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x) \,dx = 1$

Because $f_X(x) = \frac{dF_X(x)}{dx}$, we can get the CDF by integrating:

$$
F_X(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f_X(t) \,dt
$$

#### Example

Let $X$ represent the lifetime of an incandescent light bulb. After lots of experimenting, scientists found the the PDF of $X$ to be

$$
f_X(x) = \begin{cases}
\frac{1}{2}e^{-\frac{x}{2}} & x \geq 0 \\
0                           & x \lt 0
\end{cases}
$$

Now, the scientists want us to find the CDF of $X$. For $x \lt 0$, $F_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{0} 0 \,dt = 0$. For $x \geq 0$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
F_X(x) &= \int_{0}^{x} \frac{1}{2}e^{-\frac{x}{2}} \,dt \\
       &= 1 - e^{-\frac{x}{2}}
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore,

$$
F_X(x) = \begin{cases}
1 - e^{-\frac{x}{2}} & x \geq 0 \\
0                    & x \lt 0
\end{cases}
$$
