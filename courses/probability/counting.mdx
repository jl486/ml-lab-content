Many problems in probability require counting the number of outcomes of a particular experiment. Sometimes, these numbers can get unimaginably big, until even calculators can't handle them. In this lesson, we'll review some techniques for counting.

## Basic counting principles

### Multiplicative principle

The multiplicative principle states that if a process has $k$ stages with $n_1, n_2, \dots, n_k$ possible outcomes for each stage respectively, then the process can occur in $n_1 n_2 \cdots n_k$ ways.

### Factorial

The factorial of $n$ is the product of all positive integers less than or equal to $n$:

$$
n! = n \cdot (n - 1) \cdot (n - 2) \cdot \cdots \cdot 2 \cdot 1
$$

This is useful because it gives us the number of permutations of (or number of ways you can arrange) all $n$ items.

## Permutations

If you have $n$ distinct elements, and you want to choose and arrange $k$ of them, where different orders count as different outcomes, then the number of permutations is

$$
P(n, k) = \frac{n!}{(n - k)!}
$$

<MiniCollapsible>
  <MiniCollapsibleTrigger>Derivation</MiniCollapsibleTrigger>
  <MiniCollapsibleContent>
    Imagine you have $k$ blank slots that you want to fill with objects from a collection of $n$ distinct objects. You have $n$ choices for the first slot. Since $n - 1$ now remain, you have $n - 1$ choices for the second slot. For the $k$-th object, you'll have $n - k + 1$ choices. By the multiplicative principle, the number of ways we can choose objects is
    $$
    P(n, k) = n(n - 1)(n - 2)  \cdots (n - k + 1)
    $$
    Multiply the numerator and denominator by $(n - k)!$ to get
    $$
    P(n, k) = \frac{n(n - 1)(n - 2)  \cdots (n - k + 1)(n - k)!}{(n - k!)}
    $$
    The numerator is the definition of $n!$, so we finally get
    $$
    P(n, k) = \frac{n!}{(n - k)!}
    $$
  </MiniCollapsibleContent>
</MiniCollapsible>

## Combinations

The number of combinations is how many ways you can choose $k$ items from a collection of $n$ items.

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 
n \\
k
\end{pmatrix} = \frac{n!}{k!(n - k)!}
$$

<MiniCollapsible>
  <MiniCollapsibleTrigger>Derivation</MiniCollapsibleTrigger>
  <MiniCollapsibleContent>
    We can derive this by expressing the number of $k$-permutations of $n$ items in terms of combinations.

    First, we select $k$ items for the permutation. There's
    $\big(\begin{smallmatrix} 
    n \\
    k
    \end{smallmatrix}\big)$
    ways to do this. After that, we know there are $k!$ ways to permute each choice of those $k$ items. Then, we have another permutation formula:
    $$
    P(n, k) =
    \begin{pmatrix} 
    n \\
    k
    \end{pmatrix}
    \cdot k!
    $$
    Dividing both sides by $k!$ will then get us the combination formula.
  </MiniCollapsibleContent>
</MiniCollapsible>
